Mechanical Ventilation

Mechanical ventilation (MV) is a life-saving therapy It is typically instituted when a patient is unable to maintain adequate ventilation, and hence gas exchange, on their own. One of the most widely used MV techniques in intensive care units (ICU) is invasive ventilation which provides access to the lower airways through tracheostomy or an endotracheal tube (ET tube). Hence, it establishes a major advance in the management of patients with respiratory distress. In addition to ventilation management during the course of MV, extubation, or liberation from the ventilator, is considered a critical component for a successful therapy. If reintubation after extubation is required, patient recovery may be adversely affected. This can lead to worse patient outcomes, longer time of mechanical ventilation, extended length of stay, and increased hospitalization costs.

What is self-extubation?

Planned extubation refers to weaning from mechanical ventilation and removal of the endotracheal tube as planned by the medical team following well-established protocolized procedures (e.g., reduction of pressure support, spontaneous breathing trials, etc.). Nevertheless, unplanned extubation, whether accidental due to the personnel’s inappropriate manipulation of the ET tube or deliberate due to the patient’s action, is a real problem in ICUs. One study reports that the estimated incidence of unplanned extubation is around 10%, with 60% of these unplanned extubations requiring re-intubation [1]. The clear majority (62.8%–96.4%) of unplanned extubations in adult ICUs are the result of patient self-extubation [1]–[3]. This is typically attributed to patient agitation. Being intubated is uncomfortable for patients, particularly for those spontaneously breathing, and can occasionally lead them to try to pull out the endotracheal tube. While not directly linked with increased mortality, unplanned extubation has been associated with longer times on mechanical ventilation and longer ICU and hospital stay, while re-intubation is recognized as a risk factor for nosocomial pneumonia [1].
